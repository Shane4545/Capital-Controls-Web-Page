## The visual patterns that define the market baseline

The standard industry layout is not complicated. Most pages follow one of two templates. Integrator and panel-builder pages typically use **project title → short summary → background/scope/special features → contact details**, sometimes with a hero image or a small gallery. Romtec and PRIMEX are the clearest examples of that format. Manufacturer cases tend to use **hero statement → challenge/solution/result → KPI strip → product tiles/resources/video CTA**, which is the pattern visible on Schneider’s Aquapolo story and Rockwell’s case-study format. citeturn16view3turn16view6turn16view7turn15search2

The baseline image strategy is also consistent. The strongest integrator pages rely on real site photography or panel/station photos, even when the photography is not art-directed. Romtec uses a project gallery; PRIMEX uses real project photos at the top of the case; ATSYS uses a single project banner or hero image with text-heavy case structure; Valley uses one hero plus a few unlabeled image blocks. Those pages feel real because the imagery is tied to an actual project, even if the presentation is plain. citeturn16view5turn16view6turn16view7turn18search0turn18search1turn18search5

Video usage is much less mature than image usage. Xylem’s Broken Arrow page is basically a thin wrapper around a case-story video: title, short overview, then little supporting material. Rockwell’s Lake Chelan page uses video better by placing it near the top within the body narrative. Schneider’s Aquapolo page uses a top-level “Watch video” CTA in a polished story template. ABB, by contrast, pushes video into a wastewater resource hub with outbound case-study and video links rather than integrating a real project narrative on-page. The pattern is clear: video exists, but mostly as a marketing asset or resource link, not as a piece of engineering explanation. citeturn16view0turn16view1turn16view2turn16view3

The strongest pages all do three things, even when they do them imperfectly. First, they name a real owner, plant, district, or municipality. Second, they connect visuals to a concrete hardware stack — pumps, PLCs, HMIs, level devices, radios, VFDs, or controllers. Third, they use sectioned layouts that help the buyer scan quickly. Romtec and PRIMEX do this with project assets and scope sections; ATSYS does it with short problem/solution blocks; Schneider does it with challenge/solution/result tabs; Rockwell does it with image/video plus narrative. That is the current baseline for “good enough” in the niche. citeturn16view1turn16view3turn16view6turn16view7turn18search0turn18search1turn18search4turn18search5

## Where competitors still fail visually

The biggest failure is not bad aesthetics. It is **thin proof density**. Xylem’s required page is the clearest example: it tells a valid story about MultiSmart at a pumping station, but the visible page content is almost entirely a short overview around the video. There are no supporting stills of the station, panel, controller, HMI, alarm states, or installation. That is a major missed conversion asset because the visitor cannot skim the proof without pressing play. citeturn16view0

ABB has a different problem: the wastewater page is wastewater-specific, but architecturally it behaves like a hub, not a proof page. It shows one generic wastewater image, then routes visitors to case studies, blogs, application guides, and portfolio categories — including a sewage pump station level application guide — but it does not visually walk through one real wastewater control implementation on the page itself. It communicates breadth, not project-level trust. citeturn16view2

Schneider’s weakness is that its layout is stronger than its evidence. Aquapolo has good structure and unusually specific control-stack copy, but after the key figures and journey sections, the imagery shifts back to abstract promotional tiles. For a water-controls buyer, that reduces confidence because the page sounds more technical than it looks. It tells you there is a Harmony HMI and Modicon PLC logic behind the process, but it does not let you see enough of that environment. citeturn16view3

The integrator side fails in a different, more practical way: many pages are project-real but visually under-developed. Sheridan’s wastewater page has useful metrics and a clean challenge/solution layout, yet only one visible unlabeled image appears in the parsed structure. Valley’s Niagara pumping-station page is specific and honest — supplier list, scope, PLC/OIT/SCADA stack, wet-well improvements, control-room upgrades — but its images are unlabeled and the surrounding layout feels closer to a project memo than a trust-building field case. ATSYS repeatedly describes panels, PLCs, HMIs, SCADA graphics, switchboards, tags, and shutdown windows, but its pages read like concise bid recaps with a single hero image rather than a visual narrative of what was installed. citeturn16view4turn16view5turn18search0turn18search1turn18search4turn18search5

## The visual opportunities competitors are leaving open

Almost nobody is using **video as engineering proof**. The videos and video CTAs I found are mostly promotional overlays on the page, not systematic demonstrations. What is missing is a short, operator-relevant walkthrough: show the wet well, show the level device, show the panel face, show the HMI, show the lead pump starting, show the lag pump cut-in, show the alarm, show what failover actually looks like. The current market uses video to say “we were there,” not to prove “this is how the system behaves.” citeturn16view0turn16view1turn16view2turn16view3

Nobody I reviewed truly visualizes control logic in a way a non-controls stakeholder can grasp without dumbing it down. There are lists of devices and plenty of mentions of PLCs, HMIs, SCADA, tags, and telemetry, but almost no pages use a simple visual sequence to explain: normal level rise, lead start, lag start, alternating duty, high-high alarm, sensor backup, power-fail recovery, generator transfer, communications loss, or return-to-auto. That absence is why so many pages feel like “good contractor pages” rather than “best-in-industry control pages.” citeturn16view3turn16view5turn16view6turn16view7turn18search0turn18search4turn18search5

There is also a major gap around **commissioning visuals**. ATSYS comes closest in copy by talking about FAT testing, short shutdown windows, tag creation, and zero-impact cutovers, and Romtec mentions start-up/commissioning as part of its package, but the pages do not visually package commissioning as proof. There are no checklists, test screenshots, trend captures, operator handover photos, or startup punch-list evidence. For buyers in municipal infrastructure, commissioning credibility is often more persuasive than polished branding. citeturn16view6turn18search0turn18search1turn18search5

The final open lane is **proof hierarchy**. Most competitors place their strongest proof too low or disperse it across asset links, galleries, or separate videos. The opportunity is to make the first 20 seconds of the page unmistakable: real station, real panel, real screen, real outcome, real location, real scope. Right now, even many competent pages make the buyer work too hard to verify that the company actually builds and commissions the systems it describes. citeturn16view0turn16view3turn16view4turn16view6turn18search5

## How Capital Controls wins visually

Capital Controls should not try to beat this market with prettier styling. It should beat it with **evidence density**. The first screen of the page should show, simultaneously, a real pumping station or treatment asset, a real control panel, and a real HMI/SCADA view from the same job. No stock water tower. No abstract blue wave. No generic technician-with-tablet. The visual message should be: “this is the physical system, this is the control hardware, this is the operator layer.” That would immediately exceed the proof standard on the required manufacturer pages and most integrator pages reviewed here. citeturn16view0turn16view2turn16view3turn16view4turn16view5turn18search5

The image strategy should be ruthlessly specific. Use one wide environmental shot of the station or plant; one exterior control-panel image with labeled callouts; one interior panel image with wire management, VFDs, breakers, PLC, terminations, and surge/UPS/telemetry devices visible; one real HMI/SCADA screen; and one commissioning or startup image. Every image should answer a technical question. If an image cannot answer a technical question, it is filler and should not make the page. This directly addresses the weakest common pattern in the competitor set: decorative or unlabeled visuals. citeturn16view4turn16view5turn16view6turn16view7turn18search0turn18search1turn18search5

The video strategy should be short, disciplined, and demonstrative. Put one 45-to-75-second video in the hero or immediately beneath it. Do not make it a montage. Make it a system walkthrough: “Here is the wet well. Here is the level instrument. Here is the primary panel. Here is the backup/fail-safe element. Here is the HMI. Here is what operators see during normal duty and during alarm.” Then place a second, optional deeper video lower on the page showing FAT/commissioning/startup. The gap in the current market is not video volume; it is video usefulness. citeturn16view0turn16view1turn16view3

The layout strategy should make text subordinate to proof. Above the fold, the page should skew visual-first. In the middle of the page, every narrative block should sit beside an image, diagram, or screen capture. Lower in the page, the text can expand, but only where it is anchored to specific evidence such as a logic sequence, a panel callout, or a commissioning checklist. The right ratio is roughly **60% visuals / 40% text** in the top half and **50/50** through the explanatory sections. The current market often does the reverse. citeturn16view3turn16view4turn16view5turn16view9turn18search5

## Best-in-industry pumping station page blueprint

**Hero proof band.** Left side: real site photo of the pumping station, lift station, or treatment asset. Right side: real control panel or HMI screenshot. Between them: one line stating the exact system type, one line stating the owner type and location, and a four-item proof strip with pump count, sensing method, control platform, and commissioning status. The hero video lives here as a click-to-play overlay, not as a separate buried resource. This immediately beats pages that open with generic promise language or single hero shots. citeturn16view0turn16view1turn16view3turn16view6

**Problem and operating context.** A concise block explaining the actual operating problem: overflow risk, nuisance callouts, outdated PLCs, poor telemetry, lack of redundancy, difficult wet-well conditions, treatment bottleneck, or high overtime after power bumps. Beside it: one real “before” image or one screenshot of the legacy interface if available. This is where Capital Controls can visibly separate itself from vague “modernization” language. citeturn16view1turn16view4turn16view7turn18search5

**System overview with annotated diagram.** Show a simple schematic of wet well/tank/process flow, pumps, level instruments, panel, HMI/SCADA link, generator or UPS if relevant, and remote telemetry path. The diagram should be visually lighter than a P&ID but more informative than a marketing icon strip. Place it beside a one-paragraph controls summary. Nobody in the reviewed set is doing this well on-page, which makes it a high-value differentiator. citeturn16view2turn16view5turn16view6turn18search0

**Control panel proof section.** Use a two-column gallery: panel exterior, panel interior, and controller/HMI close-up. Add numbered callouts for PLC, VFDs/starters, alternation logic controller, level inputs, surge protection, telemetry/radio/cellular hardware, and backup mode. Include one sentence per callout explaining why the item matters operationally. This is the single most underused trust-builder in the niche. citeturn16view5turn16view7turn18search4turn18search5

**Sequence of operation section.** Visually explain what the controls do. Use a horizontal or stacked sequence: normal level rise, lead pump start, lag pump assist, pump alternation, high-level alarm, sensor failover, communications failover, and power-loss recovery. Pair each step with a tiny HMI or trend visual. This is where Capital Controls can become more practical than manufacturers and more credible than generic integrators. The current reviewed pages mention PLC/SCADA/HMI repeatedly, but they almost never explain the motion of the system. citeturn16view3turn16view5turn18search0turn18search5

**SCADA and operator visibility section.** Show real screen captures — overview screen, station detail, alarm banner, trend, runtime/cycle counts, and communications/health status. Add captions that say what the operator can see and what action they can take. Rockwell’s Hamilton case study proves that a real SCADA screen is a valuable proof type; Capital Controls should go much further and make it a full section, not a side image. citeturn2search10turn11search9

**Commissioning and startup proof.** Include FAT photos, IO checkout sheet excerpt, wet test or runtime trend screenshot, startup-day field image, and a short note about cutover/shutdown window. This is the section nobody is properly exploiting. It will resonate with engineers, operations managers, and procurement teams because it proves delivery competence, not just design capability. citeturn16view6turn18search0turn18search1turn18search5

**Results and operating impact.** Show four to six hard outputs: reduced overflows, fewer nuisance alarms, improved restart behavior after power issues, reduced callouts, faster troubleshooting, better visibility, or capacity increase. Use a small chart or before/after comparison, not just KPI tiles. Schneider and Sheridan show KPI blocks, but Capital Controls should anchor each result to a visible system behavior or observed operational change. citeturn16view3turn16view4turn18search5

**Close with a technical CTA, not a sales CTA.** Offer a downloadable submittal-style pack: sample panel layout, example HMI screen, sample sequence of operations, and commissioning checklist. The reviewed competitors usually end with generic contact prompts or more resource links. A controls-specific CTA would reinforce that the page is built for serious buyers, not casual browsing. citeturn16view2turn16view5turn16view6turn16view9

The winning formula is straightforward: **show the real station, show the real panel, show the real screen, show the real logic, show the real startup**.
