Website Strengths/Weaknesses
Service Clarity and Branding: Good sites immediately state their focus. For example, Actemium’s water page leads with “Delivering end-to-end automation, SCADA, and control solutions for … municipal water systems”
. Flowpoint’s panel page prominently notes “CSA / cUL Certified, making us your Custom Control Panel Shop”
. Ardea’s page similarly brands itself as a top “panel manufacturing” company
. These taglines and bullet lists make it clear they do PLC/SCADA/panel work.
Use of Real Images: The strongest pages use real photos. Trimax, Brock and others show actual facilities and control panels. For instance, Trimax’s gallery has dozens of real site images (pumps, tanks, valves)
. Sheridan’s case study uses real HMI screenshots. Ardea and Brock include real panel shop photos. These images build credibility. Even Flowpoint uses a genuine photo of an assembled panel interior
.
Explicit Services List: All sites list the same core offerings: PLC programming, HMI/SCADA design, panel fabrication, instrumentation, communications, and commissioning. Actemium’s site, for example, bullet-lists “End‑to‑End System Integration, SCADA Engineering, PLC/HMI/Network expertise…”
. Flowpoint enumerates “PLC panels, MCC cabinets, generator cabinets, pump control panels – you name it, we build it”
. This consistency sets a baseline: customers expect to see these buzzwords.
Technical Depth Variance: Few sites delve into true technical detail. Generally they describe what they do (e.g. build panels, program SCADA) rather than how. Sheridan’s case breaks this pattern by explicitly naming Ignition HMI and CompactLogix PLCs
. Most competitors stick to high-level descriptions and don’t expose control algorithms, loop strategies, or networking schemes.
Common Industry Failures
Generic Language and No Proof: Many pages are heavy on buzzwords but light on specifics. For example, Entrust Solutions’ water page spends paragraphs on “full SCADA design, HMI and PLC programming, and commissioning services”
, but contains no photos or concrete examples. It reads like a corporate brochure of services (IT/OT, data analytics, commission teams)
 without any project evidence. Similarly, sites like Automation Now or Logical Systems present dense text or stock graphics with no project shots.
Stock and Decorative Images: When images are used, they are often stock or decorative. Some sites use scenic water photos or generic diagrams that illustrate nothing specific. For instance, Intuitive Water’s homepage uses a stock mountain-lake image rather than a pump station. A few use diagrammatic illustrations (like Flowpoint’s HMI screenshots) that seem templated. Real plant and panel photos are still not the norm.
Lack of Field or HMI Screenshots: Almost none of these sites show actual HMI/SCADA screens from real jobs (Sheridan is an exception). They rarely show panels operating or connected sensors. No one is sharing the actual user interface or data graphics (except Sheridan’s case images). This means prospects don’t see what their screens would look like.
No Video or 3D Tours: None of the competitors embed actual videos. (Brock’s site has a link to a YouTube video, but it’s promotional, not a walkthrough.) There are no panel-fabrication or commissioning walkthrough videos. This is a missed chance – all sites stick to static content.
Missed Opportunities
Panel Walkthrough Videos: None provide a video tour of a real control panel or panel-building process. A short video walking through the panel shop or showing assembly would be unique.
Control Logic Explanation: Competitors do not share any of their control code or ladder logic. An interactive section or downloadable sample of pump sequencing code (lead/lag logic) could showcase expertise.
Behavioral Simulations: Explaining how the system behaves (e.g. animations of pump alternation, level control response, alarm scenarios) is absent. This kind of dynamic explanation would set one site apart.
Commissioning and Field Photos: Very few show commissioning scenes. Pictures of engineers on-site, loop checks in progress, calibration work – these are almost nonexistent. Posting photos of in-field instrument setup, sensor installation, or commissioning data sheets could greatly increase trust.
Operator Perspective: None of the pages highlight an operator’s dashboard or daily-screenflow tutorial. A guided tour video of the HMI from an operator’s viewpoint (e.g. showing alarms, trends) would fill a gap.
How Capital Controls Wins
Emphasize Real Work Over Hype: Use only genuine project photos and videos (no stock). For example, show real panels you’ve built in your shop (similar to Ardea/Brock photos
) and the actual equipment at customer sites (like Trimax does). Feature real SCADA/HMI screenshots from live projects (as Sheridan does) so visitors see what their plant’s interface could be. Include images of installed sensors (ultrasonic level sensors, pressure transmitters on tanks) and operator control rooms.

Explain the Tech: Unlike the generic competitors, Capital Controls should detail key control strategies. Spell out how pump sequencing is programmed (lead/lag control, alternation routines), how level control is achieved (float/ultrasonic sensors, PLC algorithms), and how alarms/fail-safes are handled. Provide simple ladder-logic snippets or flow diagrams and annotate them. For example, a graphic showing a flow diagram of pump alternation and high/low-level interlocks will educate and impress.

Show Commissioning in Action: Present commissioning photos or clips – e.g. an engineer “loop-checking” a new station (wiring, bench tests). This demonstrates attention to detail. Also share real commissioning data or acceptance test screenshots (e.g. a table showing measured flow vs. setpoint with all tests passed).

Incorporate Video Wisely: Have a short hero video or mid-page clip (auto-playing muted) of a pumping station startup or panel workshop walk-through. For example, 1-2 minute footage of an operator starting a station and the pumps running, overlaid with key data callouts. Video should educate, not just glamorize.

Balance Text and Visuals: While competitors often dump long text, Capital Controls should break content into scannable sections and diagrams. Use annotated photos (callouts on a panel photo labeling PLC, VFD, etc.) and infographics summarizing processes. Ensure every technical claim is backed by a visual (photo, diagram, or screenshot).

Winning Website Blueprint for Capital Controls
Hero Section (Visual Front-and-Center): Full-width photo or short looped video of a real municipal pumping station or packaged lift station at sunrise, with the title “Custom Pump Station Control Systems” overlaid. Include a tagline like “Engineered SCADA, PLC, and panel solutions for water and wastewater.”
Problem → Solution Narrative: Two-column layout: on the left, text describing common client problems (e.g. aging lift stations, manual pump switches, maintenance downtime); on the right, an annotated photo of a pump station. Below, explain “Our Solution” with bullet points (PLC upgrade, remote SCADA, panel replacement) and a photo of your control panel or panel shop.
Technical Deep Dive: Section with tabs or a visual diagram. Tabs could be “Control Architecture”, “Pump Sequencing”, “Level Control”, “Alarms”, “SCADA Integration”. Each tab contains a concise explanation plus a schematic or annotated screenshot. For example, “Pump Sequencing” tab shows a ladder logic snippet or flowchart for lead/lag control, and an actual SCADA screen of pump run-time.
System Architecture Diagram: A simple network diagram photo or drawing showing the overall system: SCADA server, PLCs, HMI workstations, panels, field instruments (sensors, transmitters). Clicking parts of the diagram could show pop-up images or notes (e.g. click pump icon to see pump panel photo).
Real Project Gallery: A horizontally scrollable gallery of high-quality photos: completed panels, installed lift stations, commissioning shots (engineers testing), on-site HMI screens. Captions name the project location or component. This visual proof shows “we’ve done it before.”
HMI/SCADA Showcase: Side-by-side screenshots of your SCADA/HMI in action (e.g. plant overview and an alarm screen). Include brief captions highlighting features (trends, color alarms). Possibly embed a 360° pan of your HMI screen.
Video Demonstration: Mid-page, embed a short (1–2 min) video showing: panel wiring or assembly, then cut to the panel powering up a station with PLC programming visible, and finally the operator interface reacting. Emphasize how the system works in real time (pump starts, level drops, etc.).
Case Study Highlight: A mini-case with a project name (e.g. “Elm Street Lift Station Upgrade”), containing before/after data or images. Include a quote from the client on performance improvement. Provide key specs (number of pumps, control method) to show real results.
Callouts and CTAs: Throughout, use pull-quotes or callout boxes with facts (“Guaranteed UL508A panels built in Ontario”, “Remote monitoring via SCADA with 99.9% uptime”). End with a strong call-to-action: “Discuss Your Pump Station Project” and easy contact info.
Visual Consistency: Use actual photos of Capital Controls hardware/work (team, shop, field). Avoid generic stock. Use color-coded diagrams or flowcharts that match your branding. Keep text short in visuals, and let photos/diagrams tell the story. Balance each paragraph with a relevant image (panel, site, screen).
By following this structure—front-loading real photos, explaining actual control logic, and showcasing the hardware and software clearly—Capital Controls’ pumping station page will appear vastly more concrete and credible than competitors’ generic content. It will look real, feel real, and prove real work to water/municipal clients.
